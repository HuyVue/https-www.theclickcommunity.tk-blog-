lifestyle photography | telling the whole story

77 Comments / Photography Tutorial, Sponsored / By Lacey Meyers
Share11
Tweet
Pin352
363 Shares

I clearly remember first joining Clickin Moms and being so surprised by how many photographers use lenses that don’t zoom. I was completely clueless about “prime” lenses and thought they sounded mighty inconvenient! Especially when one member explained to me, “You just have to zoom with your feet”.

But that train of thought has stuck with me and has now significantly shaped how I shoot. I am constantly moving, changing position and perspective. And as a primarily lifestyle photographer, I have found this to be incredibly important… you must move to tell the whole story. I use this motto each time I photograph my own kids doing an activity, but today I’d love to share with you how I went about this at a recent lifestyle family session. The goal was to capture study-time.

1.  Focus on the Subject

Establishing the subject and activity is key, and usually the first images I capture. I typically pick the best lighting angle and begin there. I also return to this position when a new activity begins.  In this case, I started here when the little boy was reading, then came back to it when he began his writing lesson.

2.  Change Perspective

Once I capture my ‘safe’ shots, I start to move around my subject as they go about their activity.  I want to be sure to get some shots that simulate the feeling of what they are seeing, too, not just what an observer would see.

3.  Don’t Forget the Details

The details are so important! On their own, these images may not have much meaning, but together with the series they play a critical role to telling the whole story. In this case, that included the little sister hiding under the table while her brother studied, the book they were reading, little sister joining in to do her own ‘studies’, and brother deciding it was time for a break.

4.  Back – it – up

This is probably the toughest one for me, because I know I am a tight shooter. But backing up and showing the scene adds so much context to your series, as well as visual interest. This will also allow you to include details in the environment. In this case, the environment is their home and details such as the cross and map on the wall are important to include as they are meaningful to this family.

Some may say that I over-shoot in situations like this, as these are only a fraction of the photos I took for this family. But when it comes to documenting cherished everyday activities, I’m not sure that over-shooting is really possible!
