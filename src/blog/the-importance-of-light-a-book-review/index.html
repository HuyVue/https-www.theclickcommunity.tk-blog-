The importance of light in photography: A book review

149 Comments / Sponsored / By April Nienhuis
Share53
Tweet
Pin9
62 Shares

Lighting is a crucial aspect of a photograph.

It is equally as important as exposure, composition, white balance, and the story being told within the frame.

I believe that great light, whether natural or artificial, is always available but knowing how to work with that light and to use it to your advantage is key.

For the first 18 months of my photography journey I had no idea that light was an important factor. But I can pinpoint the exact image and day, November 13, 2007, when I noticed how the light I used improved my overall photograph.

Ever since that day, I’ve been in love with light. Even sans camera, to watch a sun fall behind the horizon and the sky explode with brilliant colors or the beautiful contrast and richness over a landscape that you see when storm clouds break and the sun emerges.

Simply having a conversation with someone I am still watching how the light and shadows falls across their face.

Understanding light is a constant exploration and there are multiple avenues you can take to either begin or continue your knowledge. Having fellow photography friends around to bounce ideas and thoughts off of are truly invaluable but there are additional resources such as photography blogs, forums, workshops, and books.

Recently I was given the opportunity to review two new books that focus on lighting and I am thrilled to share my thoughts on them with you!

The BetterPhoto Guide to Photographing Light
by Jim Miotke and Kerry Drager
What I loved about this book is that it touches on all the basics of lighting, from the importance of proper exposure to working with various lighting situations such as high noon light and overcast light and on into modifying natural light. There is a chapter dedicated to ‘direction of light’ in which frontlight, sidelight, backlight, etc. are discussed and how they can compliment your subject. Towards the end of the book they go on to discuss HDR, infrared photography, and shooting at night. All of the chapters are simple to read and include many gorgeous photographs with detailed descriptions on each image. I love that there are special tips throughout the book and many assignments to practice what you’ve just read. Lighting is an extensive topic and not something that can be covered in one place but this is a fantastic book for wetting your palette so to speak. You’ll get a taste of all different types of lighting situations and even for someone like myself who has been in photography for 6 years now, it has filled in some gaps and rekindled my desire to shoot in situations I have put on the back burner such as night time, fog, snow, and many others.

The Luminous Portrait
by Elizabeth Messina
Elizabeth is one of my favorite photographers and I got the opportunity to hear her speak at WPPI this year. I left her class completely inspired just as I do every time I visit her website. There is something magical about her photography and getting the opportunity to peek inside her head via her new book is quite the dream. In The Luminous Portrait Elizabeth is an open book, pun intended. She discusses the challenges that come with photographing particular subjects and how she works around them such as children who don’t always want their picture taken, a woman in the late stages of pregnancy who may feel self conscious about her changing body, or celebrities and working with their assistants and time restraints. She shares what film she uses, how she interacts with clients during a shoot, thoughts on creativity, how she uses props, and how she utilizes light. Her passion for light is evident as you listen to her discuss how she sees light and uses it to create images that reflect her luminous style she is known for. The pages are not only filled with Elizabeth’s knowledge but also her stunning photography with descriptions and circumstances on those images along with her camera settings. This is an amazing book for someone wanting to see light in a different way and it has already influenced my photography.
